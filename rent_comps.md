## The Critical Finding

**The broker's "below-market" story does not survive per-square-foot analysis.**

- Windmill 1BR at **$2.10-$2.26/sqft** vs. comp average of $1.54/sqft — Windmill is **higher per sqft** than the comps.
- Windmill Studio at **$3.01-$3.21/sqft** vs. comp average of $2.74/sqft — Windmill is **higher per sqft** than every non-Broad-Ripple comp.

**Why the broker's headline number appears low:** Windmill's units are unusually small (226-506 sqft vs. 400-956 sqft for comps). Rent per unit looks like a discount because the units themselves are half the size of typical market inventory. Renters are paying premium per-sqft rent for tiny units, not a discount for renovated ones.

## Implications for Underwriting

1. **Rent-lift on renovated units is nominal.** The 5 remaining unrenovated units can be brought to renovated rent (~$50/unit lift), but the already-renovated 28 units have limited room to grow beyond submarket rent inflation.

2. **Value-add thesis is operational, not rent-driven.** Third-party PM transition, collections tightening, and R&M discipline are the actual value levers. Not rent growth.

3. **Market rent growth (2.5% underwritten) drives most of the terminal value.** Brownsburg submarket has grown 7-8% annually 2021-2023 — unsustainable. Return to Indianapolis-metro average (2-3%) is the base case.

## Caveats

- Comps for the immediate Clermont / west Indianapolis submarket are limited. Larger properties in Broad Ripple and Downtown are used as directional references, not direct comps.
- Newer construction commands premium rent regardless of location; The Landing (2023 build) is not directly comparable to Windmill (1980 build).
- Rent-per-square-foot analysis is directional, not deterministic. Absolute rent-per-unit ultimately drives affordability decisions for tenants.
